# B2B Outbound Conversation Playbook

A methodology for B2B sales reps on how to manage outbound conversations — from the first
reply through to qualified interest, a booked call, or a clean disqualification. The
principles here are product-agnostic. Concrete examples throughout are drawn from one
specific case (a rep selling sales-automation software) and are clearly marked as
**Illustrations** — they show the principle in action, they are not scripts to copy-paste
into your own conversations. Adapt the pattern, not the words.

---

## Core Philosophy

Every reply — positive, neutral, or negative — is an opportunity. The goal of each message is to:

1. **Move curiosity → interest → need** (never skip stages)
2. **Never hard-pitch** — position your product naturally, in context of their situation
3. **Ask one focused question at a time** to maximize reply rates
4. **Always plan the next move** (the follow-up) before hitting send
5. **Mirror the prospect's tone, energy, and vocabulary**
6. **You have three shots — play them wisely**
7. **Never add mental burden** — every message should make it easier to say yes, not introduce new doubts or problems

## Before You Reply: Research First

Before writing anything, check the prospect's LinkedIn profile and company page:

- **Job title + description**: What do they actually do day-to-day? Read the full experience section, not just the title.
- **Company size + industry**: Is your solution actually relevant at their scale and in their vertical?
- **Target audience**: Who are *they* selling to or serving? (Their customers shape their pains.)
- **Tech / vendor signals**: What tools, partners, or platforms do they mention? What does their stack imply about their maturity and priorities?
- **Vocabulary**: Note the exact words they use in their profile — you'll mirror these back in your message.
- **Team size and structure**: Check the "People" tab on their company page. The shape of the team (lots of SDRs, lots of CSMs, heavy engineering, etc.) tells you what's core to their business.

> You often get more value from the LinkedIn description and experience section than the title alone. Scroll down.
> Check the company's "People" tab — the team's shape tells you what the company actually does and what they prioritise.

**Why this matters**: Research lets you craft questions that feel informed rather than generic, and lets you use the prospect's own words back at them (mirroring). It also tells you whether it's worth investing time in this lead at all.

**Bottom-up prospecting**: Don't ignore individual contributors or lower-level reps as leads. They feel the pain directly and can give you intel on the team's situation and frustrations. From there, you can move up to their manager or decision-maker with informed context.

## The Conversation Funnel

```
Curious reply → Explore their situation/pain → Soft position your solution → Build need → Demo/call
```

At each stage, keep asking yourself: **"Does my next message move them closer to understanding my solution's value?"**

**The core goal — find their problem:**
Every message should be oriented around surfacing a deep-seated problem. Details about their current setup (tools, processes, vendors) are the *end goal* of your research — not a question to open with. Think like a good discovery call: your job is to find what's keeping them up at night, then show how your solution addresses it.

A rough heuristic for what's top-of-mind by company stage:

| Company size | What's probably keeping them up at night |
|---|---|
| Very early stage / pre-PMF | Signing the first customers, finding product-market fit |
| Growing (10–50 people) | Efficiency — making the most of a small team's time |
| Established (50+) | Optimization, scale, hitting quota/targets consistently |

**Don't break the conversational flow.** When replying, don't take a sharp left turn to a generic question. Stay in the thread of what you've been discussing, acknowledge their situation, and pivot naturally. If you suddenly switch topic (e.g., "What tools are you using?"), it signals you're not listening — just executing a script.

> ✅ Acknowledge → Stay in flow → Pivot toward their problem
> ❌ Ignore what they said → Generic question → Feels interrogative

## The Three-Shot Rule

**You have a maximum of three meaningful exchanges with a prospect before they stop reading.**

After three messages without value, the prospect will mentally categorise you as noise — and any future message, no matter how good, will be ignored. This is why every message must be intentional.

**Implications:**
- If your second shot is stronger than your first, consider skipping straight to it.
- Don't waste a shot on "did you see my last message?" — that's not a shot, it's noise.
- Plan all three shots in advance before you send the first one.
- The "reason I'm asking" follow-up counts as a shot — make it add real value.

**Shot sequencing pattern:**
1. **Shot 1** — Open question to explore their situation or surface a pain.
2. **Shot 2** — "Reason I'm asking" + a specific piece of context (a visual, a case, an observation about their setup) that builds curiosity about your solution.
3. **Shot 3** — After 1–2 weeks: light humour, bring back their original pain, put the ball in their court.

## Core Rules for Writing Replies

### 1. One Question at a Time
Asking multiple questions = lower reply rate + longer replies = lower chance of response.
Pick the **most important question** for where you are in the funnel. Save the others for later exchanges.

### 2. Ask Open-Ended Questions
Avoid yes/no questions when possible — if a prospect answers "no," you're stuck.

> **Illustration (sales-automation rep):**
> ❌ "Are you using N8N for outreach?" → risk of dead-end "no"
> ✅ "What are you using N8N to connect your enrichment stack with?" → forces elaboration

The pattern: turn a closed question about *whether* they do something into an open question about *how* they do it.

### 3. Always Plan the Follow-Up Before Sending
Before hitting send, ask: *"If they don't reply, what do I write next?"*
The follow-up structure is: **"The reason I'm asking is [context that adds value or shows research]."**

**Important nuances on "reason I'm asking":**
- It doesn't have to start with those exact words — it's a framework, not a template.
- It must add new context, not just rephrase the question. A reformulation means your original question wasn't clear enough.
- The best "reason I'm asking" is specific to them: reference their team size, their target audience, their setup — show you've done the work.

> **Illustration (sales-automation rep):**
> ❌ "The reason I'm asking is to see if [product] could work for you." → vague, no value
> ✅ "The reason I'm asking — you have 19 SDRs targeting CSMs who are very active on LinkedIn. I'd have assumed you'd be automating that." → specific, shows research

**The "reason I'm asking" as a quality check on your first question:**
Sometimes when you write out your "reason I'm asking" follow-up, you realise it's actually *better* than the question you were about to send. That's your cue — skip the first question entirely and lead with the stronger version.

> **Illustration:** You were going to ask "What tools are you using?" but your "reason I'm asking" is "You're targeting CSMs who are very active on LinkedIn — I'd be surprised if you're not using it as a channel." The second is stronger. Lead with it.

**Don't default to generic opener questions like "what's your stack?"** Asking for someone's tools, process, or team structure cold is a low-signal question. It's a fallback when you haven't done research. The goal is to surface a *problem* first — details about their setup will emerge naturally once they're engaged. Generic openers treat the prospect as a data source, not a conversation partner.

**When they mention two problems at once:** Focus on one at a time — don't try to address both simultaneously. Pick the one that's most likely to lead somewhere useful for your solution and follow that thread. Save the other for the next exchange if needed.

> **Illustration:** A prospect says "Not easy to meet client expectations" and "our tool stack isn't performing." Pick one. If you try to address both, your message gets long, unfocused, and harder to reply to.

**Never pitch before you know the problem.** If you find yourself writing a paragraph about how great your product is without knowing what the prospect's actual struggle is, stop. You're randomly pitching. The prospect has no frame of reference for why they should care. Find the problem first — then your product becomes the logical answer.

### 4. Mirror the Prospect's Style
Match their energy, formality, length, and vocabulary:
- Formal long message with signature → match it
- Three-word casual reply → keep it short too
- Emoji user → you can use them too
- Technical vocabulary (APIs, endpoints, etc.) → use technical language back
- **Use the exact words from their LinkedIn profile or messages** — if they say "outbound engine," use "outbound engine." If they say "spray and pray," mirror it back.
- Direct, no-greeting tone → skip "Hi [Name]," and get straight to it

### 5. Don't Position Your Product Too Early
Your product should enter the conversation naturally — ideally when:
- The prospect mentions a pain you can solve
- They ask what you do / what you use / how you'd approach it
- You're sharing context (your own setup, a customer's situation) to build rapport

### 6. Keep Messages Short and Scannable
If a prospect is closed off or the message is a follow-up, keep it to 2–3 lines maximum.
A long message to a reluctant prospect will go unread. Earn length with engagement.

### 7. Use Strong, Specific Words
Avoid weak filler language:
- ❌ Vague umbrella terms ("low value tasks," "streamline your process") → ✅ Specific activities ("manual follow-ups, reminders on LinkedIn")
- ❌ The same noun repeated twice in a sentence ("tools that integrate with your tools") → rewrite it
- ✅ Mirror their own words: if they said "reduce complexity," use "reduce complexity"

## Positioning Your Product Without Pitching

There are several patterns for introducing your product naturally. Pick the one that fits *your* product, *your* role, and the specific moment in the conversation. Don't force a pattern that doesn't suit your situation.

### Pattern A — The "Personal Setup" Move (when applicable)
If you personally use the product you sell, share your own setup as if giving advice, not selling. This creates trust because you're a practitioner, not just a vendor.

> **Illustration (sales-automation rep who uses their own product):**
> "I use [product] for pretty much my whole process — enrichment, LinkedIn + email outreach. Drastically simplified things."

**This only works if you genuinely use the product.** If you sell something you don't personally use (infrastructure, industry-specific tools, enterprise software), skip this pattern and use one of the others.

### Pattern B — The "Similar Customer" Move
Reference a customer with a situation similar to the prospect's. The prospect recognises themselves in the story, without you pointing a finger at them.

> **Illustration:**
> "We work with a few agencies in the 15–20 person range doing exactly this — the shift that changed things for them was [specific outcome], because [mechanism]."

Specific beats generic. "A team like yours" is weak; "a 15-person agency targeting mid-market SaaS" is credible.

### Pattern C — The "Observation" Move
Share an observation about the prospect's setup, category, or situation — then let them respond. You're not pitching, you're pattern-matching out loud.

> **Illustration:**
> "Most teams I talk to with [specific setup] end up running into [specific friction] around month three. Curious if that's been your experience."

### Pattern D — The "Mirror Their Pain" Move
Use something you know about their stack or situation to plant a seed about the gap your product fills:

> **Illustration (sales-automation rep, knows they use a tool that lacks enrichment):**
> "Handling enrichment natively has been a huge time saver for us — no more juggling [tool] for that separately."

This only works if your research is solid and the pain you name actually matches their situation.

### Pattern E — The Visual Artifact Move
When a prospect is curious but not yet hooked, or when words alone would feel too salesy, show instead of tell. The visual artifact can be:
- A screenshot (of a dashboard, a sequence, a report)
- A short Loom or video walkthrough
- A one-pager or slide from a case study
- A diagram or architecture sketch

Frame it as *"Actually, this is what I had in mind"* — mysterious, not pushy. One image or short clip can convey in seconds what a paragraph can't.

Works best as **Shot 2** when words alone would be too salesy, or the prospect is technical / already equipped with something and needs to *see* the difference, not hear it described.

> **Illustration (sales-automation rep sharing a sequence screenshot):**
> "The reason I'm asking is to see whether [product] could reduce complexity for your process too. Actually, this is what I had in mind [screenshot]."

### When a Competitor Is Mentioned
Don't call them a "direct competitor" — it signals a sell and puts the prospect in defensive mode.
Instead: acknowledge the competitor respectfully, then pivot to curiosity about *how* the prospect uses them.

> **Illustration:**
> ✅ "[Competitor] is a seriously good contender to us."
> Then pivot: "Though it looks like [Company] is more B2C — what's the strategy you're using them for?"

The pivot is the key move. Respect the competitor, then use your research to ask an informed question that reveals whether the fit is actually right for them.

## Handling Negative or Cold Replies

**Rule: Never disqualify without confirmation. Always work the reply.**

Even a rude or dismissive reply can be turned around if context supports it. Steps:

1. **Acknowledge briefly** — match their directness, don't over-apologise
   - If they're blunt: `Got it.` then move forward
   - If they pushed back on tone: `Sorry if that came across wrong.`
   - Skip "Thanks for your reply" with direct prospects — it reads as filler

2. **Explain your reasoning** using their specific signals (what you saw on their profile, their company, their public posts)

   > **Illustration:** "I noticed you were following [specific page] and running a lead gen agency, so I assumed outreach automation was central to your stack."

3. **Use their own profile vocabulary** in your recovery. If their profile says "outbound engine for clients" → use "outbound engine for clients" in your question.

4. **Leave a soft, specific open door** — one informed question, not a generic one

   > **Illustration:** "I saw you're doing LinkedIn + email outreach and you hate spray and pray — which tool are you using to automate that?"

5. **If confirmed negative**: Close gracefully and archive

   > **Illustration:** "Sorry if I wasted your time. Wishing you luck with [their specific context — B2C, early stage, new market, etc.]."

> Only truly disqualify when the prospect explicitly confirms a clear reason: they're happy with their current solution, not doing the activity your product addresses, confirmed wrong target, or there's genuinely no fit.

## Moving to a Demo or Call

When a prospect is **hot** (showing interest, asking questions, or explicitly willing to learn more):

- **Don't wait** — suggest a call directly. If they're hot, a calendar ask will land.
- **Assess the lead first**: Is the account large enough to be a real opportunity? If it's very early stage or below your ICP, it may still be worth taking the call for practice — but manage your own expectations.
- **Adapt the conversation to their stage**: Early-stage / pre-PMF companies may not be doing the activity you're pitching yet, but could have an adjacent need. Help them think about their specific situation and show how your solution applies to that, not to a textbook case.
- **Frame the call as useful for them**, not just a demo:

  > ✅ "I think it'll be easier to assess what you need and see if there's a fit."
  > ❌ "I'd love to show you a demo of [product]."

- **Don't drop the calendar link immediately** in the same message as the call ask — it feels transactional. Ask for the call first; only send the link if they don't reply:

  > Follow-up if no reply: "Just wanted to make sure you saw this — if it's easier, here's my link: [calendar]. Book whenever works."

### Handling Multi-Identity Handoffs
When outreach is sent from one person (e.g., you) but the actual call would be with someone else (a specialist, an AE, a senior rep):

- **Always explain the handoff** — don't just pass them over silently, it feels like a brush-off.
- Frame the specialist warmly, give a reason they're the right person.
- Use "together" or "with you" language to keep it collaborative, not transactional.

> **Illustration:**
> "How about I arrange a call with our early-stage specialist, [Name], next week? She works specifically with teams at your stage, and together you'll be able to assess what you need and see if there's a fit."

## Follow-Up Timing & Snooze Logic

| Signal Level | Timing |
|---|---|
| Demo request / inbound interest | Immediately |
| Hot — direct question, ready to engage | Same day |
| High curiosity / active engagement | 1 day |
| Moderate curiosity / light engagement | 3 days |
| Open but not hot | 1 week |
| Negative but worth one recovery | 1 week |
| Closed off — used Shot 2, waiting for Shot 3 | 2 weeks |
| Confirmed disqualification | Archive |

**Day-of-week strategy:**
- Best days to send follow-ups: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
- Avoid Monday (inbox chaos) and Friday afternoon (pre-weekend, low engagement)
- If a snooze would land on a bad day, manually pick a specific date
- Don't ask permission to come back later — just snooze and come back when it makes sense

## Qualifying the Prospect

During conversation, verify:
- **Are they doing the activity your product addresses?** If yes, manually or with another solution?
- **What's their current setup?** (Tools, process, vendors, team structure.)
- **Where is the friction?** (What step breaks down, what takes too long, what costs too much?)
- **Is the account big enough to matter?** (Team size, revenue band, maturity stage.)
- **Does their audience / use case fit your ICP?** (If not, be honest about it.)
- **What stage are they in?** (Early-stage may not be doing structured work in your area yet.)

> Individual contributors (SDRs, specialists, operators) are valid leads: they feel the pain and can give you intel to take up the chain.
> If the person you're talking to isn't in-scope (wrong role, wrong team), confirm briefly before moving on.

### Matching your message to the persona's level

When you get a reply, check: **Is this person the one actually using the thing you're selling, or are they too senior?**

A VP or SVP at a 100+ person company is probably not the hands-on user. If your hook was about a tactical detail (a tool they follow, a specific activity), they may genuinely not know what their team is doing at that level. Don't ask them for operational details they don't have — acknowledge the mis-hook, use their seniority as a conversation point, and try to get the name of the actual practitioner in their team.

> **Illustration (sales-automation rep who hooked an SVP of Sales via a Clay follow):**
> ❌ "What tools are you using for outreach?" (wrong — they're not the user)
> ✅ "My bad — I guess the SVP of Sales isn't the one building lists for a team of 30+ salespeople. Though I'm curious — is your team using LinkedIn as an automated channel? If not, they're probably leaving a lot on the table."

If they confirm they're not the right person, ask for the name of who is:

> **Illustration:** "Though I really think we can help — who's running outreach in your team?"

### Checking if a prospect has previously engaged with you

If you can see in your CRM that a prospect was previously in a trial, a demo, or a sales cycle, **use this as a second-shot move**. Don't mention it on the first reply — save it as the follow-up context:

> **Illustration:** "I actually noticed you tested [product] back in [date] but didn't go with us — what did you end up using instead?"

This works because: it shows you've done your homework, it's an honest question, and it skips directly to what you need to know (what they adopted, and implicitly why you didn't stick).

### Wrong persona — when to redirect vs. dig deeper

If you realise mid-conversation that the person isn't the fit you thought (e.g., you hooked them thinking they were a user, but they're in a different role), don't give up immediately. Ask yourself:
- Why did your hook resonate with them in the first place?
- Is there an adjacent use case that still fits?
- Can you still find a problem worth exploring?

If you genuinely can't find a fit, apologise briefly and ask for the right contact:

> **Illustration:** "Sorry, I think I got it wrong — who in your team is running [the activity]?"

## Message Structure Cheat Sheet

**Standard reply flow:**
1. Mirror opener (match their greeting energy and directness)
2. One line of rapport / acknowledgement
3. Soft product mention if context allows (your setup, a similar customer, an observation)
4. One open-ended question that moves the conversation forward
5. [Before sending] Plan Shot 2 (reason I'm asking) and Shot 3

**Negative reply flow:**
1. Acknowledge briefly — match their tone, skip pleasantries if they did
2. Explain your reasoning using their specific profile signals
3. One specific, informed question using their own vocabulary
4. [Before sending] Prepare Shot 2 as a visual artifact or direct transparency move

**Hot lead / call ask flow:**
1. Validate their insight or reply
2. Brief positioning (1–2 lines max, specific value tied to their situation)
3. Suggest a call framed as useful for them
4. If multi-identity: explain the handoff warmly before sending the specialist's name

## Handling Objections Before Pushing a Trial or Demo

**Never push the trial/demo until you've resolved the objection and confirmed the need.** This is one of the most common mistakes — jumping to "did you sign up?" before the prospect has a reason to sign up.

The right sequence before any trial ask:
1. Resolve any expressed objection (pricing, complexity, relevance, timing)
2. Confirm the need (understand what problem they want solved)
3. Confirm urgency (why now, not in 6 months?)
4. *Then* suggest the trial or demo

### Price objections
When pricing is mentioned as a concern, don't ignore it and push the trial anyway. Surface what they actually saw and understood:

> **Illustration:**
> "Why do you say it's scary?" or "What did you see on the pricing page?"

Let them explain what they understood — because they likely didn't see the full value (what's included, what isn't, total cost of ownership vs. their current setup). Once you understand their misread, you can reframe it. Only then does the trial make sense to them.

### Adding mental burden is a deal-killer
Every time you say "keep in mind that X" or "just so you know, Y could be a problem," you're giving them a reason *not* to try. Be a problem-solver, not a problem-adder. Information that's correct but premature belongs in onboarding, not the sales conversation.

> **Illustration:**
> ✅ "Perfect — I can set you up with [specialist] when you're ready."
> ❌ "Sure, we can do that — though keep in mind that deliverability and reply rate will be much higher with a business email..." → you've just introduced a new problem

### When a prospect says they "want to try" but you haven't confirmed the need yet
Don't ask "did you sign up?" — you'll ask it every week and they'll ignore you, because they have no real reason to sign up. Instead, go back and establish the need:

> **Illustration:** "You seem excited about testing it out — what's the use case you want to try with us?"

Only once you understand the use case (and have validated that your product solves it) do you push the trial. If you skip this, you'll end up chasing someone who was never really going to convert.

## Handling Former Customers

When you discover someone previously used your product (via CRM research or from them mentioning it directly), this is a high-value opening — use it strategically.

**Don't close them off just because they moved away.** The right move is to find out:
1. **When** did they leave? (Look up in CRM)
2. **Why** did they leave? (Direct question, non-judgmental)
3. **What has changed** since then that addresses that reason?

Then position your evolution:

> **Illustration:**
> "I see you were using us back in [date] — we've made a lot of improvements since then on [specific area they cared about]. What made you leave at the time?"

If they left for a valid reason that's now fixed, you can win them back. If they loved it but simply didn't continue, give them an update on what's new:

> **Illustration:** "Since you loved it — we've added [feature] since you left, which sounds right up your alley."

**Low-value-lead signals.** Prospects who optimise heavily for cost rather than outcomes — chasing discounts, lifetime deals, self-hosted alternatives, patchwork stacks — are unlikely to convert to a paid relationship. Recognise the profile quickly and don't invest too many shots. Archive if it's clearly not a fit, but extract intel (what they're using, why they chose it) before you do. That information is useful context for the next prospect in that segment.

## LinkedIn vs. Email Conversations — Key Differences

The same core principles apply to both channels, but the context of *where* the prospect is when they read your message matters:

| | LinkedIn | Email |
|---|---|---|
| Mindset when reading | Slacking off, scrolling, open to chat | Working, goal-oriented, being interrupted |
| Reply rate | Higher — people are more relaxed | Lower — you're an interruption in their workflow |
| Reply quality | More casual, back-and-forth friendly | Often more formal, considered |
| Best for | Curiosity building, conversation development | Good lead who's already warm — push for call quickly |
| Formatting | Short, no bullet points | Can use structure, bullet points, signature mirror |

**Practical implications:**
- Expect the majority of your replies to come from LinkedIn — keep that tab open and aim to be reactive
- Email replies are rarer and more considered — if someone replies by email and they seem like a good lead, lean toward booking a call faster rather than building curiosity over multiple exchanges
- **Mirror the email format**: if they have a signature, use one; if they write four words, write four words; if they're formal, be formal
- **Replying quickly on LinkedIn works in your favour** — you catch them while they're still "in the mood" (scrolling, not in task mode)

**Inbox hygiene:** Snooze everything that's waiting so you reach inbox zero and always know when a new reply lands. Being reactive on LinkedIn is one of the highest-leverage habits you can build.

**Reactivity is a competitive advantage.** When a prospect replies on LinkedIn, they're often live on the app. If you can reply within minutes, you catch them while they're still in the conversation mindset — it feels like a live chat and builds a much stronger connection. A reply that took 3 days will feel like a cold restart. Keep the LinkedIn tab open and treat new replies as priority interrupts.

**Prioritise warm/active conversations first.** Don't work your inbox top-to-bottom. Leads who are actively engaged and replying fast should jump the queue. Leads who are snoozed can wait. A backlog of unanswered warm replies is the worst outcome — you lose the momentum when it matters most.

**Treat it like a conversation, not a form.** The best replies come from asking yourself: *"If I were on the phone with this person right now, what would I say?"* The answer is almost always simpler, shorter, and more human than what you'd type in a message. If you're overthinking your reply, imagine speaking it out loud first.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- ❌ Asking multiple questions in one message
- ❌ Mentioning your product too early (before pain is established)
- ❌ Asking self-centered questions (useful for you, not for them)
- ❌ Giving up on negative replies without exploring the profile
- ❌ Using vague umbrella terms ("optimize your workflow," "automated outreach") instead of specific activities
- ❌ Calling a competitor a "direct competitor" — it signals a sell
- ❌ Ignoring reply tone/length when mirroring
- ❌ Sending without planning the follow-up
- ❌ Using generic "reason I'm asking" that doesn't show research
- ❌ Repeating the same question in a follow-up (it signals your first message wasn't clear)
- ❌ Starting "Thanks for your reply" with a blunt prospect — reads as filler
- ❌ Using weak or repetitive language (same noun twice in one sentence, generic umbrella verbs)
- ❌ Sending a calendar link in the same message as the call ask — wait for the follow-up
- ❌ Doing a silent identity handoff without explaining who the specialist is and why
- ❌ Sending follow-ups on Monday or Friday afternoon
- ❌ Burning more than three shots without a clear strategy
- ❌ Asking permission to come back later — just snooze and come back
- ❌ Defaulting to "what's your stack?" as an opener — it's a fallback for when you haven't done research, not a real shot
- ❌ Breaking conversational flow with a sharp left turn to a new topic — acknowledge where they are, then pivot
- ❌ Wasting a shot on an obvious yes/no question where you already know the answer
- ❌ Asking a senior leader for operational/tactical details they don't have — they're not the user
- ❌ Asking "am I right?" instead of "did I assume wrong?" — the latter is humble, the former reads as cocky and kills replies
- ❌ Spacing follow-up shots too close together — give at least a few days between each
- ❌ Pushing for a trial or demo before resolving a pricing objection
- ❌ Asking "did you sign up?" before confirming the need — you'll ask it every week and they'll ignore you
- ❌ Adding mental burden (telling the prospect about potential problems before they've committed)
- ❌ Writing a pitch paragraph before you know the prospect's problem — you're randomly pitching
- ❌ Using AI-generated or AI-sounding language in conversational messages — if it reads like a press release, it will be ignored
- ❌ Writing messages that require reading three times to understand — keep it simple enough to skim on a phone
- ❌ Working your inbox top-to-bottom — always prioritise warm/active conversations over cold snoozed ones
- ❌ Asking two slightly different versions of the same question in one message
- ❌ Not being reactive on LinkedIn — if they reply and you take days, you lose the live-conversation momentum

## Pattern Library (with Illustrations)

The phrases below are organised by **pattern** — the structural move the phrase is making. Each pattern comes with at least one illustration from the sales-automation case. **Do not copy these illustrations verbatim into your own conversations.** They show what the pattern looks like when it's specific and well-researched. Your version should use *your* product name, *your* customer's vocabulary, and *your* research — never theirs.

### Rapport / Icebreaking
**Pattern:** Acknowledge something they said, then briefly share a related experience of your own — without pivoting into a pitch.

> **Illustration:** "I hear you with [tool] — hated the pricing change. Luckily I didn't rely too much on it since I use [product], which includes that natively."

**Pattern:** When they see through your move but stay engaged, own it with humour.

> **Illustration:** "Ha, you caught me 😄"

### Soft Positioning
**Pattern:** Share what you do (or what your product does) as a matter-of-fact setup choice, not a recommendation.

> **Illustration:** "I drastically simplified my LinkedIn + email outreach by using [product]."

**Pattern:** Tie your product to a specific time-saving mechanism, not a vague benefit.

> **Illustration:** "Most notably, [product] handling enrichment natively was a huge time saver."

### Moving the Conversation
**Pattern:** Reference something specific they said, then ask them to expand on the part that matters for you.

> **Illustration:** "You mentioned wanting to simplify your stack — which part are you looking to optimize?"

**Pattern:** Express genuine curiosity about a choice they made, especially one that seems counterintuitive.

> **Illustration:** "I'm curious though — why did you decide to scale down on automation?"

**Pattern:** Ask how they connect two specific things in their setup.

> **Illustration:** "What are you using [tool A] to connect your [tool B] with?"

### Negative Reply Recovery (direct tone)
**Pattern:** Acknowledge the brush-off in kind, state your inferred conclusion, then leave one specific open door.

> **Illustration:** "Got it. I guess you're happy with the tools you're using to operate your outbound engine for your clients. Though I'm curious — you're doing LinkedIn + email outreach and you hate spray and pray. Which tool are you using to automate that?"

### Negative Reply Recovery (softer tone)
**Pattern:** A brief, non-grovelling apology + an explanation of the specific signal that led you to them.

> **Illustration:** "Sorry if that sounded off. I noticed you were following [specific page], so I thought one of your portfolio companies might be doing [specific activity] — was that too far-fetched?"

### Competitor Softener
**Pattern:** Acknowledge the competitor's legitimacy, then pivot to a research-informed question about whether the fit is actually right for them.

> **Illustration:** "[Competitor] is a seriously good contender to us. Though it looks like [Company] is more B2C — what's the strategy you're using [Competitor] for?"

**Pattern:** Play slight underdog, but probe for dissatisfaction.

> **Illustration:** "Sounds like you've got LinkedIn + email figured out with [Competitor]. It's going to be a rough one for me 😅 — are you 100% satisfied with them though?"

### Wrong-Persona / Wrong-Hook Recovery
**Pattern:** Own the mis-hook explicitly, explain what you saw and what you assumed, then ask a new question that fits their actual role.

> **Illustration:** "My bad — I saw that you followed [tool] so I assumed you were a user. But obviously the SVP of Sales isn't the one building the list for a team of 30+ salespeople."

**Pattern:** Ask humbly whether you got it wrong — "did I assume wrong?" invites a reply, "am I right?" reads as cocky and kills replies.

> **Illustration:** "Did I assume wrong?"

### Acknowledging a Tough Situation
**Pattern:** Name their situation as tough, then immediately ask what they're going to do about it — keeps them in problem-solving mode.

> **Illustration:** "Wow, sounds tough. What's the next step — double down on [current approach] with a different angle, or going for new markets?"

### When They're a Former Customer or Trial User
**Pattern:** State what you found, ask what they replaced you with, non-judgmentally.

> **Illustration:** "I actually noticed you tested [product] back in [date] but didn't go with us — what did you end up using instead?"

### Visual Artifact / Curiosity Move
**Pattern:** Pair "the reason I'm asking" with an artifact that shows more than it tells. Frame it as mysterious, not pushy.

> **Illustration:** "The reason I'm asking is to see whether [product] could reduce complexity for your process too. Actually, this is what I had in mind [screenshot]."

### Call Ask
**Pattern:** Frame the call as useful *for them* — a way to assess their need — not as a demo of your thing.

> **Illustration:** "How about we jump on a call next week? I think it'll be easier to assess what you need and see if [product] fits."

### Multi-Identity Handoff
**Pattern:** Warmly introduce the specialist with their role and why they're the right person. Use "together" language.

> **Illustration:** "How about I arrange a call with our early-stage specialist, [Name], next week? Together she'll be able to assess what you need and see if [product] fits for you."

### Follow-Up If No Reply to Call Ask
**Pattern:** Low-friction nudge with the link this time, not before.

> **Illustration:** "Just wanted to make sure you saw this — if it's easier, here's my link: [calendar]. Book whenever works."

### Shot 3 — After 1–2 Weeks, Light Humour
**Pattern:** Self-deprecating framing + return to their original pain + put the ball in their court.

> **Illustration:** "I shot and missed, I guess — it's not the [part you pitched] part you need simplification on. What is it?"

### Closing a Confirmed Disqualification
**Pattern:** Brief apology + specific well-wish that shows you understood their situation.

> **Illustration:** "Sorry if I wasted your time. Wishing you luck in your B2C endeavors — I know it can be tricky."

### Pricing Objection Handling
**Pattern:** Surface their specific concern before addressing it — don't assume you know what they meant.

> **Illustration:** "Why do you say it's scary?" / "What did you see on the pricing page?"

### Re-Engaging After Uncovering Wrong Context
**Pattern:** Show you understand their frustration by pattern-matching to similar customers, then ask a calibration question.

> **Illustration:** "I feel you — I have agencies in my portfolio that complain about the same situation. I'm guessing they're expecting a high reply rate. How high, and what do you usually get?"

### When They Say "Not Our Main Focus Right Now"
**Pattern:** Treat the statement like a phone call — probe naturally for the reason underneath.

> **Illustration:** "How come? You got enough leads from other channels?"
> **Illustration:** "Is outbound not your main source of opportunities right now?"

### When They Indicate Buying Intent but Haven't Confirmed the Use Case
**Pattern:** Name the enthusiasm, then redirect to the use case before the trial.

> **Illustration:** "You seem excited about testing it out — what's the use case you want to try with us?"

### Surprise / Curiosity Move When They're Doing Something Manually
**Pattern:** State your surprise as a statement, not a question. Let the statement do the work and wait for them to explain themselves.

> **Illustration:** "Oh, I'm surprised — you seem technical. I'd have thought you'd have automated that tedious part already."
